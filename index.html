Century of Change

Overview & The Islamic Empires

Definition of the Century Starts: January 1, 1801
Ends: December 31, 1900
Early 19th Century Change
  • Slavery was abolished.
  • The First and Second Industrial Revolutions (overlapping with 18th & 20th centuries) triggered massive urbanization.
  • Resulted in higher levels of productivity, profit, and prosperity.

The Gunpowder Empires (16th–18th C.)

Also known as the "Era of Islamic Gunpowder Empires," referring to the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires.

  • Among the strongest, most stable economies of the early modern period.
  • Led to commercial expansion and patronage of culture, art, and architecture.
  • Saw rising per capita income, population growth, and technological innovation.

Geography: Centralized from Europe/North Africa in the West to Bangladesh/Myanmar in the East.

Shifting Global Powers

Collapse & New Influence

The Islamic Gunpowder Empires were formally dissolved as European imperialism swept in, bringing South Asia, Southeast Asia, and nearly all of Africa under colonial rule.

The Great Collapse

This era was marked by the collapse of major powers:

  • Spanish Empire
  • Zulu Kingdom
  • First French Empire
  • Holy Roman Empire
  • Mughal Empire

This paved the way for the growing influence of:

  • British Empire
  • Russian Empire
  • United States
  • German Empire
  • Second French Empire
  • Kingdom of Italy
  • British Habsburgs
Post-Napoleonic Expansion After defeating the French Empire and its Indian allies, the British and Russian empires became the world's leading powers. Russia expanded into the Caucasus, Central Asia, and the Far East.

Decline of Indian Powers

Remaining powers in the Indian subcontinent suffered massive decline. This included:

  • Kingdom of Mysore and its French allies
  • Nawabs of Bengal
  • Maratha Empire

Result: Marked the Company's dissolution. India was ruled directly by the British Crown (The British Raj).

Rapid Imperial Expansion

  • First half of the century: Rapid growth in Canada, Australia, South Africa, and heavily populated India.
  • Last two decades: Rapid expansion in Africa.

By the century's end, Britain controlled 1/5th of the world’s land and 1/4th of its population.

Pax Britannica

"British Peace" (1815-1914)

Latin for "British Peace," spanning from 1815 (End of Napoleonic Wars) to 1914 (Start of WWI).

It ushered in an unprecedented globalization and economic integration on a massive scale.

What Defined It?

  • British Naval Supremacy The Royal Navy was unmatched, controlling sea routes and suppressing piracy and the slave trade.
  • Global Trade Expansion Britain promoted free trade, built global markets, and established London as a financial center.
  • Colonial Expansion The empire became the largest in history, expanding dramatically in Africa and Asia.
  • Diplomatic Influence Britain acted as a global mediator to prevent major European wars.
Economic Growth Fueled by the Industrial Revolution. Britain exported capital, technology, and goods. Railways, telegraphs, and steamships connected the empire.
Political & Cultural British legal, political, and educational systems spread. English became a global language. Global norms in trade and diplomacy were influenced.
Limits & The End Peace was enforced by control, not agreement. Colonial conflicts were frequent (exploitation/cultural suppression). The era ended with the rise of Germany, the US, and Japan, culminating in WWI (1914).

Industrial Revolution

Origins, Drivers & Transformation

Begun in England (late 18th C) and accelerating through the 19th C, this made Britain the world's first industrialized nation.

Key Drivers
  • Abundant coal and iron resources.
  • Stable political system & expanding empire.
  • Tech Innovations: Steam engine (James Watt), spinning jenny, power loom.
  • Growth of capitalist enterprise & banking.

Economic Transformation

  • Textiles Shifted from cottage industries to mechanized factories (Manchester, Leeds).
  • Iron & Steel Production surged, fueling construction and railways.
  • Transport Revolution in canals, railways, and steamships connected markets and reduced costs.
  • Agriculture Enclosure Acts and mechanization displaced rural workers, pushing them to cities.

Urbanization

Population & Living Conditions

Population Explosion

Between 1801 and 1901, England’s population exploded from 9 million to over 30 million. Rural populations migrated to cities like Liverpool and Birmingham for factory work.

London 1m ➝ 6m
Manchester 75k ➝ 351k

Living Conditions

Urbanization brought overcrowding, poor sanitation, and disease. Working-class neighborhoods were often:

  • Densely packed slums with inadequate housing.
  • Polluted by coal-burning factories and open sewage.
  • Vulnerable to epidemics (Cholera, Typhoid).

Reformers: Women like Octavia Hill and Josephine Butler campaigned for better housing and public health.

Social Impact

Class, Education & Literacy

Class Restructuring

  • New Middle Class Emerged from merchants, professionals, and factory owners.
  • Working Class Faced long hours, low wages, and dangerous conditions.
  • Labor Movements Trade unions began organizing to demand rights and reforms.

Education & Literacy

The need for skilled labour and civic participation led to expanded education.

Result: Literacy rates rose. Newspapers, novels, and public lectures became widespread.

Literary Responses

Dickens & Engels

Charles Dickens

Vividly criticized industrial society. In Hard Times (1854), he portrays "Coketown," a grim industrial city where utilitarianism and mechanization suppress imagination and compassion.

  • Characters: Stephen Blackpool (factory worker) and Sissy Jupe (circus girl) represent the human cost.
  • Condemned: Dehumanizing factory life, poor education, and rigid social hierarchies.
Friedrich Engels

In The Condition of the Working Class in England (1845), he offers a sociological critique based on his time in Manchester.

  • Describes appalling living conditions, child labor, and exploitation.
  • Laid the foundation for Marxist theory (industrialization creates class conflict).
  • Viewed industrialization as a human tragedy provoking resistance.

Victorian England

Values, Reform, Class Structure & Empire

Victorian Values

Morality & Duty

  • Values rooted in moral rectitude, self-discipline, and social responsibility.
  • Respectability: Central to identity. Encompassed modesty, sobriety, and adhering to norms.
  • A respectable individual was industrious, pious, and family-oriented.

Gender Roles

  • Men: Providers and public figures.
  • Women: Idealized as "Angel in the House" (moral guardians of the home).
  • Sharply defined roles for public vs. private life.

Religion & Progress

  • Evangelical Christianity: Influenced public behavior (Temperance Society, Sabbatarianism).
  • Patriotism: British Empire viewed as a "civilizing force".
  • Industrial success was seen as a moral triumph.

Class Structure

Upper Class

Aristocrats, Landed Gentry, Wealthy Industrialists

  • Held political power, owned vast estates, inherited titles.
  • Children educated privately; sons entered politics/military/clergy; daughters groomed for marriage.

Middle Class

Merchants, Professionals, Managers

  • Grew rapidly due to industrial capitalism.
  • Valued education, respectability, and upward mobility.
  • Led reform movements.

Working Class

Factory Workers, Miners, Servants, Labourers

  • Majority of the population.
  • Lived in overcrowded housing, long hours, limited healthcare/education.
  • Women and children worked to supplement income.

Reform Movements

Labour & Factory Reform

  • Factory Acts (from 1833): Regulated child labor, limited hours, mandated inspections.
  • Lord Shaftesbury: Campaigned for humane conditions for women and children.

Public Health & Sanitation

  • Public Health Act (1848): Sanitation, clean water, urban planning.
  • Edwin Chadwick: Argued disease was rooted in poverty/poor living conditions.

Education & Political

  • Education Act (1870): Compulsory schooling (ages 5-13). Tool for moral improvement.
  • Reform Acts (1832, 67, 84): Expanded franchise.
  • Chartist Movement: Demanded universal male suffrage (failed short term, laid groundwork).

Women's Rights

  • Campaigns for property rights, education, suffrage.
  • Josephine Butler: Fought against Contagious Diseases Acts (which targeted women unfairly).

Industrial Cities Comparison

Manchester

"Cottonopolis"

  • Heart of global cotton industry.
  • Driven by steam-powered textile mills.
  • Symbol of progress and inequality (harsh labor).

Birmingham

"Workshop of the World"

  • Metalworking, small manufacturing, engineering.
  • Produced "toys" (buttons/pins), bicycles, steam engines.
  • Pioneered division of labor (complex production chains).

Literary Reflections

Writers captured the moral tensions and class struggles:

  • Charles Dickens: Criticized industrial society.
  • Elizabeth Gaskell: Captured class struggles.

The British Empire (Overview)

One of the most expansive and influential empires in history. From early mercantile ventures to its height in the 19th century and decline in the 20th, it was marked by complex governance, ideological contradictions, and persistent critiques.

Model Answers

20 Key Questions for Revision

01

Give rise to the Indian Rebellion of 1857

The cause was the massive decline of local Indian powers like the Kingdom of Mysore, the Maratha Empire, and the Sikh Empire.

Their dissatisfaction with the British East India Company’s rule led directly to the rebellion. The result was the end of the Company and the start of the British Raj, where India was ruled directly by the British Crown.

02

Give rise to the growth of the British Empire

The British Empire grew fast in the early 19th century by taking territories in Canada, Australia, South Africa, and India.

Later, it expanded heavily in Africa. By the end of the century, the Empire controlled one-fifth of the world’s land and one-quarter of its population.

03

Write on British Indian relationship during the 18 and 19 centuries (6 paragraphs)

Early on, India was home to the Mughal Empire, one of the "Gunpowder Empires." It had a strong economy and advanced technology.

By the 19th century, remaining Indian powers like the Kingdom of Mysore, the Maratha Empire, and the Sikh Empire suffered a massive decline.

During the Napoleonic Wars, the French had allies in India. When the French were defeated, these Indian allies lost their support, allowing Britain to take control.

Dissatisfaction with the British East India Company grew among the Indian people. This tension caused the outbreak of the Indian Rebellion of 1857.

After the rebellion, the Company was dissolved. The relationship changed as India came under the direct rule of the British Crown, known as the British Raj.

Under the Pax Britannica, India became a key part of the Empire. The British introduced their own legal and education systems to enforce control and economic integration.

04

What is the role of Octavia Hill and Josephine Butler?

Octavia Hill was a reformer who worked for better housing and public health to help people living in poor slums.

Josephine Butler campaigned for women's rights. She fought against the Contagious Diseases Acts, which treated women unfairly, and she challenged strict Victorian gender rules.

05

What did Charles Dickens depict in the Hard Times novel?

In Hard Times, Dickens criticized industrial society. He described a fictional town called Coketown, which was a grim and dirty city.

He showed how utilitarianism and machines destroyed human imagination. His characters, like the worker Stephen Blackpool, represented the suffering caused by industry.

06

What did Charles Dickens do in his own literary works in 1854?

In 1854, Dickens published Hard Times. In this book, he attacked the bad effects of factory life, poor education, and the gap between social classes.

He used his writing to show how the focus on profit and facts in places like Coketown was crushing human kindness.

07

What is meant by Marxist theory?

Marxist theory is a political idea arguing that industrialization created class conflict and inequality.

It comes from the work of Friedrich Engels, specifically his book The Condition of the Working Class in England, which described how workers were exploited by capitalism.

08

How can we describe the Victorian age?

The Victorian age (1837–1901) was a time of great transformation under Queen Victoria.

It was defined by industrial expansion, imperial dominance, and social upheaval. While there was progress, there were also strict moral values and a rigid class structure.

09

What is meant by Princely states?

Princely states were semi-independent regions in India, such as the state of the Nizam of Hyderabad.

During the 19th century, these states declined in power alongside other empires like the Sikhs and Marathas, eventually falling under British control.

10

What is meant by Evangelical Christianity?

Evangelical Christianity was a religious belief that deeply influenced public behavior in the Victorian era.

It tried to make society follow Christian ethics. This led to movements like the Temperance Society, which discouraged drinking alcohol.

11

What is meant by Reformers?

Reformers were people who wanted to fix the problems caused by progress, like disease and unfair labor.

Important reformers included Lord Shaftesbury (factories), Edwin Chadwick (sanitation), and Josephine Butler (women's rights). They fought for laws to improve life for the poor.

12

Write in detail on Defeat of Napoleon in 1815 (6 paragraphs)

The defeat of the French Empire in the Napoleonic Wars was a turning point. It ended French dominance in Europe.

After this, the British and Russian empires became the world’s leading powers.

The Russian Empire used this chance to expand into the Caucasus and Asia.

For Britain, defeating Napoleon meant defeating his Indian allies (like Mysore). This secured British power in India without French interference.

The end of the war started the Pax Britannica (British Peace), a period of global stability led by Britain.

During this time, Britain used its Naval Supremacy to enforce global trade and economic integration.

13

Describe the literary movement during the 19 and 20 centuries

Writers during this time responded to the problems of industrialization. They captured the moral tensions of society.

Charles Dickens wrote novels to criticize the loss of humanity in factories. Friedrich Engels wrote non-fiction about the bad conditions of the working class. Also, more people learned to read, so newspapers became very popular.

14

What are the main types of social reform introduced by Queen Victoria?

  • Labour Reform: The Factory Acts limited working hours and stopped child labor abuse.
  • Public Health Reform: The Public Health Act of 1848 built sanitation systems and provided clean water.
  • Education Reform: The Act of 1870 made school compulsory for children aged 5–13.
15

Discuss two religious movements in English society

  • The Temperance Society: Influenced by religion, this group told people to stop drinking alcohol to be more moral.
  • Sabbatarianism: This movement insisted that Sunday (the Sabbath) must be strictly kept as a day of rest and religion.
16

What is the main role of men and women?

Men: They were expected to be providers and work in public.

Women: They were seen as the "angel in the house." Their role was to stay home and be the moral guardians of the family.

17

Discuss the main reform movement which took place during the Victorian era

  • Chartist Movement: Demanded voting rights for men.
  • Factory Reform: Lord Shaftesbury fought for safer working conditions.
  • Sanitation Reform: Edwin Chadwick fought for clean water and sewers in slums.
  • Women's Rights: Early feminists fought for education and property laws.
18

What are the three main Victorian values?

  • Morality: Being religiously good and following Christian ethics.
  • Duty: Having self-discipline and responsibility toward society.
  • Respectability: Being modest, sober, and hard-working.
19

Describe these literary works' impact on the life/society

These works exposed how hard life was for the poor.

  • Dickens: Showed the dehumanizing effects of factories, making people feel sympathy for workers.
  • Engels: His book laid the groundwork for Marxist theory by explaining class conflict.
  • General Impact: Because literacy rose, these books helped people understand the need for social reform.
20

What is meant by Respectability?

Respectability was central to Victorian identity. It meant being modest, sober, and following rules.

To be respectable, a person had to be hard-working, religious, and focused on family. This was the standard set by the middle and upper classes.
